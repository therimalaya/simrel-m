5.3 Rotation of predictor space

In order to generate predictor variables \((\mathbf{x})\) and response variables \((\mathbf{y})\), we construct matrices \(\mathbf{R}\) and \(\mathbf{Q}\) and use them for orthogonal rotation of their respective principle components \(\mathbf{z}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\). This defines a new basis for the same space as is spanned by the principle components. In principle, there are many possible candidate of rotation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\) and \(\mathbf{Q}\). Among them, the eigenvector matrix of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{xx}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{yy}\) can be an option. However, in this reverse engineering both rotation matrix and the covariance matrices \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{xx}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{yy}\) are unknown. So, we are free to choose any \(\mathbf{R}\) and \(\mathbf{Q}\) that satisfied the proportion of a real valued rotation matrix, i.e \(\mathbf{R}^{-1} = \mathbf{R}^t\) and \(\mathbf{Q}^{-1} = \mathbf{Q}^t\) so that \(\mathbf{R}\) and \(\mathbf{Q}\) are orthonormal. Further there determinant becomes \(\pm 1\).

Among several methods (Anderson, Olkin, and Underhill 1987; Heiberger 1978) of generating random orthogonal matrix, in this paper we are using the \(\mathcal{Q}\) matrix obtained from QR-decomposition of a matrix filled with standard normal variates. The rotation here can be a) restricted and b) unrestricted. The later one rotates all principle components together and makes all predictor variables somewhat relevant for all response variables. However, the former one performs a block-wise rotation so that it rotates certain selected principle components together. This gives control for specifying certain predictors as relevant for chosen response. This also lets us to simulate irrelevant predictors which can be detected during variables selection procedure.
